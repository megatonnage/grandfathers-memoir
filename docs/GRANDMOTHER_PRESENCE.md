# Grandmother's Presence

## The Challenge

She did not write. Her presence in these memoirs lives in the memories of her children and grandchildren. She was a loving, comforting presence, with a gentle sense of humor. She was transparent in her emotions—never hiding her sympathies and frustrations—but always clearly loving.

How do we honor someone who exists between the lines?

---

## Possible Approaches

### 1. Audio — The Chorus of Remembrance

Recordings of family members sharing memories of her. Her laugh, her voice in others' stories. A "Chorus of remembrance" that runs parallel to the text.

- Collect audio from children, grandchildren
- Transcribe for accessibility but keep audio as primary
- Place at chapter transitions or alongside relevant moments

---

### 2. Images as Text

Her hands, her kitchen, her garden. Photos treated with the same reverence as written words.

- Hover reveals quotes from family about what she was *doing* in that moment
- Photo essays as interstitial chapters
- Her presence in the background of grandfather's photos

---

### 3. Pattern/Texture — The Visual Motif

A recurring visual element that appears throughout the site:

- Her favorite fabric pattern
- The color of her teacups
- Jasmine flowers (scent made visible)
- Something you *feel* before you notice

---

### 4. Negative Space

Intentional gaps where she should be:

- A paragraph that trails off
- An empty chair in a photo
- A silence in the audio
- The absence as presence

---

### 5. Interstitial Moments

Between chapters, small meditations:

> "Your grandmother would have been fishing here."
> 
> "She made this soup when it rained."

Fragments of her life as punctuation.

---

## Open Questions

- What do you remember most vividly about her?
- Are there photos, objects, or spaces that embody her presence?
- Would family members be willing to record memories?
- What was her relationship to the memoirs—did she read them? Encourage them?
